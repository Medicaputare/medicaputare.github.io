AI in Therapeutic Drug Monitoring: Opportunities & Challenges

Advancements in optimizing drug dosages and ensuring patient safety through intelligent systems

Rephaim Mpofu November 10, 2025

Understanding Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM) is the clinical practice of measuring specific drug concentrations in a patient’s blood to maintain a therapeutic range that maximizes efficacy and minimizes toxicity. It is especially critical for medications with narrow therapeutic indices, variable pharmacokinetics, or serious toxicity potential.

Traditionally, TDM has relied on pharmacokinetic (PK) models and manual interpretation by clinical pharmacologists or pharmacists. However, the increasing availability of electronic health data, coupled with advances in artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML), is transforming how we approach dosing and drug monitoring.

The Role of AI and Machine Learning

AI-driven models can analyze large, complex clinical datasets to predict optimal drug concentrations and dosing intervals in real time. These systems learn from historical cases, adjusting for variables such as age, renal function, genetic profile, and comorbidities — all within milliseconds.

Machine learning algorithms outperform traditional PK equations in adaptability. They can continuously refine their predictions as more data becomes available, leading to safer, more precise dosing decisions.

Case Example: Vancomycin Dosing and Monitoring

Vancomycin is an antibiotic used to treat severe infections caused by resistant bacteria, such as MRSA. Its efficacy and toxicity are highly concentration-dependent, and achieving the right exposure (AUC/MIC ratio) is crucial.

AI-based TDM models have shown great success in optimizing vancomycin dosing. Instead of relying solely on static trough-level targets, machine learning systems integrate patient data — including renal function trends, body weight, infection severity, and previous dosing outcomes — to predict individualized dosing regimens.

Studies demonstrate that AI-assisted dosing achieves therapeutic targets faster, with fewer dose adjustments and lower nephrotoxicity rates. For busy hospitals or under-resourced clinical settings, this translates into improved outcomes and workflow efficiency.

Benefits of AI-Driven TDM

  • Precision: Algorithms can consider complex nonlinear relationships among clinical variables beyond what manual dosing equations capture.
  • Efficiency: Real-time computation reduces turnaround times for dose adjustments, especially in critical care environments.
  • Scalability: Cloud-based or embedded models can serve multiple healthcare facilities, enabling remote support and harmonized clinical standards.
  • Learning Capability: Continuous retraining with new data improves predictive accuracy over time, adapting to evolving patient demographics or microbial resistance patterns.

Challenges and the Path Forward

Despite the promise, integrating AI into TDM workflows faces challenges. These include data standardization, interoperability with hospital systems, model validation across diverse populations, and clinician trust in algorithmic recommendations.

Moreover, regulatory frameworks for clinical AI tools are still maturing. Ensuring explainability — where clinicians understand the rationale behind AI-generated doses — remains essential for safe adoption.

To overcome these barriers, collaboration between clinicians, data scientists, software developers, and regulators is key. Solutions must be transparent, evidence-based, and adaptable to local healthcare contexts.

Accelerating Deployment and Clinical Impact

The deployment of AI-assisted TDM tools can be accelerated through integration with existing clinical decision support (CDS) platforms and electronic health records. Cloud-based APIs, containerized dosing models, and lightweight mobile dashboards allow rapid scaling across hospital networks.

By embedding these tools within clinician workflows — not as replacements but as intelligent aids — healthcare systems can unlock the full potential of precision dosing.

The Role of Pharmacogenomics in Personalized Medicine

Understanding genes to revolutionize drug therapy

Medicaputare November 7, 2025

Understanding Pharmacogenomics

Pharmacogenomics (PGx) is the study of how genetic variations influence an individual’s response to medications. It combines pharmacology — the science of drugs — with genomics, which explores the structure and function of genes across the entire genome.

 By understanding how specific genetic differences affect drug metabolism, efficacy, and toxicity, clinicians can make informed decisions that improve treatment safety and effectiveness. Instead of a “one-size-fits-all” approach, therapy becomes genuinely personalized.

Genomics vs. Pharmacogenomics

Genomics looks broadly at all of a person’s genes and how they interact with each other and the environment. Pharmacogenomics focuses specifically on genes that influence drug response — often those encoding enzymes such as the CYP450 family, transporters, and receptors.

For example, some people metabolize drugs too quickly (reducing efficacy), while others metabolize them too slowly (increasing toxicity). Pharmacogenomic testing helps predict these responses before a drug is prescribed.

Clinical Examples

Warfarin: Safer Dosing Through Genetics

Warfarin, a commonly prescribed anticoagulant, is effective but challenging to manage. Its dose requirements vary widely, and small differences can lead to bleeding or clotting. Variants in the CYP2C9 and VKORC1 genes strongly influence how warfarin is metabolized and how sensitive a patient is to the drug. Pharmacogenomic testing allows for genotype-guided dosing, reducing the risk of complications and hospitalizations while improving therapeutic stability.

Tamoxifen: Optimizing Breast Cancer Therapy

Tamoxifen is used in oestrogen receptor–positive breast cancer. However, it must be converted by the enzyme CYP2D6 into its active form, endoxifen. Patients with reduced CYP2D6 activity produce less endoxifen, decreasing the drug’s benefit. Pharmacogenomic testing can identify patients with these variants, allowing oncologists to tailor therapy — such as using alternative medications — to achieve better outcomes.

Hypertension: Is there a role for a precision medicine approach?

Hypertension management is another area where PGx holds promise. Genetic differences in pathways such as the ACE, ADRB1, and CYP3A5 genes can affect response to ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and calcium channel blockers, respectively.

Through genetic profiling, clinicians may be able to predict which classes of antihypertensive drugs will be most effective and least likely to cause side effects — a major step forward for personalized chronic disease management in diverse populations like South Africa’s.

Adoption and the Future of Personalized Care

With genetic testing costs falling and integration with electronic health systems improving, pharmacogenomics is rapidly moving from research to routine clinical care. However, success requires collaboration across healthcare systems — between clinicians, laboratories, insurers, and technology partners.

As adoption increases, patients will benefit from safer prescriptions, fewer adverse drug reactions, and more effective treatments — bringing the vision of personalized medicine to life.
